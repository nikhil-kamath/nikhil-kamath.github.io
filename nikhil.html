nikhil kamath

Nikhil is a sophomore undergraduate at the University of Maryland. As a computer science major, he is exploring hardware-software interaction. Local to the DMV area, he enjoys exploring a range of restaurants from Asian fusion to traditional American, from street foods to finer dining. He likes cooking diverse cuisines in his apartment, getting the most enjoyment out of Mexican and Indian dishes.

I wrote a Japanese Renga, a traditional extension of the Haiku depicting my experience with a Wren, a Japanese fine-dining restaurant in the heart of Tysons, VA. The primary motivation behind this work was to explain how we often overly focus on “fitting in” to a restaurant's demographic instead of appreciating the restaurant's food and feel. It depicts my first visit, feeling out of place among the corporate workers and the loungey, mature company as a college student. The structure of a Renga is supposed to be alternating "Haiku" blocks with their classic 5-7-5 syllable structure, along with "Mora" blocks with 7-7 syllable structures. Traditional Renga poetry is done by alternating the authors of stanzas, allowing for a collaborative experience. However, since I could not convince anyone to write this poem with me, it is hand-written by myself.

I wrote a traditional American narrative on Call Your Mother, a popular and experimental bagel chain popular in DC. Its motivation was to give a more informal description of DC’s whimsical and flowery side, typically undermined by the uptight, federal regions. It’s written from the perspective of me trying to convince a UMD student reader about the appeal and ease of visiting Call Your Mother from College Park. It leaves the reader with a more general remark about how expensive food in the DC area is getting, even for something as simple as a bagel. American poetry comes in many distinct forms with little required structure. I wrote this as a narrative with a strict AABB rhyme scheme. Its 7-stanzas are split by the fourth, middle stanza, which follows an AAAA rhyme scheme. The first half of the poem is a third person description, and the second half is a first-person reenactment.

I wrote a Mexican-Spanish sonnet on Chipotle, a popular Mexican fast-food chain. The poem’s exigence is to appeal to the general population’s opinion that Chipotle’s servings are dwindling, with most of the volume of their bowls and burritos coming from rice. It describes the initial motivator behind why I began cooking Mexican food in the first place.
